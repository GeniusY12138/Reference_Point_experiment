In this part, you will be presented with 32 different choice screens. On each of these screens, you will be presented with a list of choices for you to make. The first option will be the same in each question: it will be an amount of money that you will receive today, plus the chance of receiving some money in one month's time. The second option will change in each question.

Here is an example. In this section, I am going to ask you the following list of questions:

Lottery A is 40% chance of $20.00, and 60% chance of 0. Every option on the left hand side is the same: Today: $1.00, One Month: Lottery A. The option on the right gets better as you move down the screen.

Q# Option I Option II
1 Would you rather have: Today: $1.00, One Month: Lottery A or Today: $1.00, One Month: $0.01
2 Would you rather have: Today: $1.00, One Month: Lottery A or Today: $1.00, One Month: $0.02
…
1,999 Would you rather have: Today: $1.00, One Month: Lottery A or Today: $1.00, One Month: $19.99
2,000 Would you rather have: Today: $1.00, One Month: Lottery A or Today: $1.00, One Month: $20.00

In each question, you pick either Option I or Option II (the money). After you answer all 2,000 questions, and if this screen is selected for payment, I will randomly pick one question and pay you the option you chose on that one question. Each question is equally likely to be chosen for payment. Obviously, you have no incentive to lie, because if that question gets chosen, then you'd end up with the option you like less.

I assume you’ll choose Option I in at least the first few questions, then switch to Option II. To save time, I will ask you at what dollar value you would switch, i.e., the first line you would choose Option II. I will then fill out your answers for all 2,000 questions based on that switch point.

On each choice screen, I will directly ask you at which dollar value you would switch.
